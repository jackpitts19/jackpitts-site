Five cities in two years

Chicago is where I grew up. Suburbs, sports, a normal Midwestern childhood. Then I left for Nashville, for Vanderbilt, and for two years after that I didn't really stop moving.

Nashville taught me the classroom was the smaller part of the education. I studied Human and Organizational Development, started a nonprofit called Trash Troopers, and met people I'm still close with. But even senior year, I already knew I wasn't staying in Tennessee once it ended.

So I went to London for four months. No real plan beyond wanting to live somewhere unfamiliar for a while. Different pace, different rooms, nobody who already knew me. I came back convinced that the fastest way to learn anything about yourself is to get uncomfortable somewhere you have zero history.

Then New York, for private equity — three firms in a few years, Blue Wolf Capital, Coastal Partners, Kingfish Group. That's its own kind of restlessness, but it's also how you learn fast: different desks, different partners, different lessons about how deals actually move. I loved that city more than I expected to.

Here's what four cities in two years actually taught me. I wasn't chasing something new each time. I was running from standing still. Comfort made me anxious; new rooms didn't. That's a useful instinct at twenty-two and a dangerous one to build an entire life around.

So coming back to Chicago wasn't the easy option, it was the deliberate one. Staying, on purpose, in the city I already know, is the first time in five years I picked a place instead of just leaving the last one. Next part gets written back home.

— Jack Pitts
