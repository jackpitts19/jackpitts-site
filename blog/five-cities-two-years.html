Five cities in two years

Chicago is where I grew up — suburbs, football and basketball, a normal Midwestern childhood. Then I left for Nashville, for Vanderbilt, and for two years after that I didn't really stop moving.

The real education in Nashville wasn't in the classroom, it was building a nonprofit called Trash Troopers from nothing and learning how to keep people showing up to something that didn't exist a year before. I studied Human and Organizational Development on paper. Off paper, by senior year, I already knew I wasn't staying in Tennessee once it ended.

So I went to London for four months, no plan beyond wanting to live somewhere unfamiliar for a while. Nobody there had known me longer than a few weeks, and I liked that more than I expected to.

Then New York, for private equity — three firms in a few years: Blue Wolf Capital, Coastal Partners, Kingfish Group. That kind of turnover is its own restlessness, but it's also how you learn fast, watching different desks move deals differently. I liked that city too.

Here's what five stops in two years actually taught me: I wasn't chasing something new each time, I was running from standing still. Comfort made me anxious; new rooms didn't. That works fine at twenty-two. It stops working as a plan.

So coming back to Chicago was on purpose, not a fallback. Staying in the city I already know is the first time in five years I've picked a place instead of just leaving the last one. Next part gets written back home.

— Jack Pitts
