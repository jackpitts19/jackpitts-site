Any idea, any AI

Here's the old rule, the one I grew up assuming was just how building worked. You could have the best idea in the room, but if you couldn't code it yourself, the idea wasn't worth much until you found someone who could, or raised enough money to hire them. Permission, capital, a technical co-founder — that was the toll booth between an idea and a real thing. Most good ideas never made it through.

That toll booth is gone, or close enough to gone that it doesn't matter anymore. If you understand your idea deeply enough — the actual mechanics of the problem you're solving, not just the pitch — you can now build it yourself. Not a mockup, not a slide deck. The real thing, working.

HelmIQ is the proof, because I lived it. We didn't have a software team. We had a CRM that didn't fit how we actually ran deals, a pile of spreadsheets, and a hundred follow-ups living in someone's head. The old version of me would have written a spec, gone looking for an engineer, and waited. Instead we built it ourselves, because building it ourselves had suddenly become something two guys running an M&A firm could actually do. Not because we became engineers overnight. Because we understood the problem better than anyone we could have hired to solve it, and AI closed the gap between that understanding and working software.

I don't think that's a small shift. It means the person with the sharpest read on a problem — the owner living inside it every day — is now also the person best positioned to fix it, instead of having to hand that job to someone further away from the problem than they are. That's a real reallocation of who gets to build, not a productivity tip.

I'm genuinely excited about this, not in the abstract, future-of-everything way people talk about AI. I'm excited because I've now done it, on a real problem, with real stakes, and it worked. If you actually understand your idea, there's no excuse left for not building it.

— Jack Pitts
