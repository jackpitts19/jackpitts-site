Imposter syndrome, loneliness, and feeling blessed

Right now it's just me and Harvey. I moved back to Chicago to build this, and most days that means an apartment, a laptop, my dog, and a list of things to get through before I let myself stop. Nobody tells you this part when you decide to build something instead of join something. It's quiet. It's a lot of hours alone with a dog for company, and that's most of what my life actually looks like right now, whatever it looks like from the outside.

Most of my friends are further along in a version of life I'm not living at the moment — steady jobs, relationships that have settled in, weekends with plans made two weeks out. I'm not saying that to put any of that down or to suggest I'm ahead of it. I'm not. I picked a different shape for this stretch, and the honest cost of that is fewer nights out, fewer people who understand what I'm actually doing day to day, and some real distance from friends I used to talk to constantly. Some weeks that distance is the hardest part of any of this.

Underneath that is the part I don't have a tidy answer for. Between Salt Creek Advisory and HelmIQ, there are days I'm convinced I'm just good at sounding like I know what I'm doing. I'll be confident on a client call, and then it ends and I sit there wondering whether I actually earned the seat or just talked my way into it. I haven't resolved that. I don't think it resolves. I think you just keep working anyway.

Here's what's still true on the hard days, though. Nobody handed me this, and nobody's coming to save it if I stop showing up. That's a heavy thing most mornings, and it's also exactly why I get to be here — alone in Chicago, dog at my feet, doing the work on my own terms. Lonely right now, and genuinely blessed to be building this anyway.

— Jack Pitts
