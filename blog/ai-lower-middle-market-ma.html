How AI is actually changing lower-middle-market dealmaking

Yes, but not in the way most of the hype suggests. AI isn't finding your next deal or closing it for you — it's removing the busywork around the deal so you can spend more of your time actually doing it.

Where it actually helps

At Salt Creek Advisory, the parts of the job that used to eat the most hours were never the calls with owners. They were the upkeep: keeping a pipeline current, remembering who said what and when to follow up, and making sure nothing important lived only in someone's head. That's exactly the gap HelmIQ was built to close, and it's the part of the process where AI genuinely earns its keep:

  • Keeping companies, contacts, and notes organized and current in one place, instead of scattered across a spreadsheet and someone's memory.
  • Drafting follow-ups and prep so the reminder and the rough first pass are already sitting there when you need them.
  • Keeping every deal and every stage visible, so nothing slips because it wasn't written down.

None of that is glamorous. All of it used to cost hours a week that are better spent on the phone with an owner.

Where it doesn't replace judgment

The parts of this business that actually matter are still entirely human. Sourcing runs on relationships you build over years, not a database query. Negotiation is reading a room, not running a model. And the single hardest thing in a sell-side process — figuring out why an owner is really selling, what they're actually afraid of, what they need to hear to trust you with thirty years of their life's work — is not something software does. I don't think that changes. I don't want it to.

What this means if you're doing deals right now

Use AI for the organizing and the busywork, and protect the time that frees up for the parts only a person can do. If a tool is helping you source better relationships or read an owner more clearly, keep it. If it's just automating admin, that's still worth it, but don't confuse it with the actual work. The firms that get this right in the lower middle market over the next few years won't be the ones with the most AI. They'll be the ones who used it to buy back the time to be better at the human part of the job.
